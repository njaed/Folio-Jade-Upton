•Week One•

From the moment I caught sight of Mumbai from the plane window, I began my journey of what would be two weeks of never-ending culture shock. Expecting the iconic city of vibrant colour I was told so frequently stories of, I was instead met by a sleeping city cloaked beneath a smothering blanket of grey smog. I sat with my mouth ajar in disbelief that the dystopian artistic depictions of irreversible climate change I’ve been fed so frequently by liberal Australian media was suddenly so real and right in front of me. This continued on the bus ride from the airport to the hotel as we chaotically drove past informal settlements held together by tarps and scrap metal, rivers saturated with more rubbish than there was water, and structures that I couldn’t tell were being built or demolished. I couldn’t help but find symbolism in these observations that mirrored the state of India’s political leadership and its apparent ignorance towards sustainable development; legendary beauty being its sacrifice. However within the first week, I realised that I had no real knowledge or right to make such an ignorant assumption, and that Mumbai’s beauty dances alive, rich and complex in its unique chaos.

During our first day at TISS we learnt about India’s experience of British colonisation, a piece of history I knew of vaguely but did not realise how significant of an effect it had on India’s present day. I assumed that because India’s indigenous population gained back sovereignty and independence from British rule, they do not face the same present day ramifications as Australia’s Aboriginal population who still face great systematic injustices as a result of colonisation progressing to the extent it has today; and are ultimately still deprived of their rightful sovereignty. This assumption was wildly incorrect to say the least. Indeed, Britain had horrifically “removed India from its position of the world’s manufacturing powerhouse to a symbol of poverty and famine” during the mid-nineteenth and twentieth centuries (Tanya Koshy, 2022). And certainly, the immense economic and social repercussions resultant from the injustices of colonialism are still visible today- though of course India and Australia’s experience of post-colonialism differs incredibly based on their unique history.

What really imprinted the reality of these repercussive social issues upon me were the conjunctive experiences outside of the classroom. At TISS we learnt thoroughly about the ways in which current social issues such as gender inequality and mass urbanisation plays an important and greatly overlooked factor in the climate change crisis. Gaining this knowledge and then walking the streets of Mumbai and talking to locals put this knowledge into the perspective of the individual experience; opposed to being fed statistics and generalised experiences, I could hear the different lived perspectives of these social issues and create an understanding based upon how locals perceive these issues. I found this to be far more valuable than a judgement based upon my own understanding of morality with regards to this complex intersection, as it made me understand that the ethics and morality of problem solving must always be dependent upon the views and ways of living of a local population (Partha Mukhopadhyay and Aromar Revi, 2011) .

Particularly, visiting the home-grown neighbourhood of Dharavi illuminated how empowering and listening to local voices is the most ethical and effective way to assist in the ratification of the issues they face. At Dharavi I learnt that the inhabitants of Dharavi are proud of their work, where they live, and of their community. They do not want the operations of their community to change. Paired with this experience, I learnt from TISS that informal settlements such as Dharavi are incredibly efficient in generating economy with minimal effects upon the environment compared to other factory based industries. The problem is not necessarily the existence of informal settlements but rather the lack of occupational health and safety policies that ensures safe working conditions. Hence, according to the people who I spoke to in Dharavi, the government must prioritise including local voices in “mitigation” and “adaption” initiatives (Azman Usmani, 2022). Currently, it is common that marginalised demographics populate informal settlements and are subject to government action that is ignorant of their way of living, and forces them to assimilate to new ways of living that are not suited to their lifestyles and ways of generating income, ultimately further disempowering them (Madhushree Sekher, 2022).














•Week Two•




Over week one we learnt in great detail the different perspectives that are available to view climate change within the context of Mumbai and India in general. Throughout week two, we started shifting this focus to the practicalities of actualising solutions to remedy both climate change and the social issues that exacerbate its potency among our allocated groups.

My group and I realised that we had different focuses that we would like to pursue from what we had learnt over the week- mine personally being exploring the positive effect empowering local voices has upon the experience of climate change within rural villages. What I tried my best to do in workshops was facilitate conversations that allowed everyone to express their passions which would enabled us to find an issue that incorporated all of our interests. By establishing our three main priorities (Climate Action, Quality Education and, Reduced Inequalities) together as a group, we realised that the issue of climate induced vulnerability within minority communities combined a lot of our passions and permitted us to pursue our various areas of interest.

However, these differences did not end here. A debate developed regarding how we should frame the issue to best depict the issue. This concerned how we should address marginalised communities, particularly women, without the threat of generalisation; a fear instilled into me by Rebecca Pearse’s “Gender and Climate Change”. Pearse stresses that “gendered vulnerability to climate change is not due to characteristics intrinsic to women as a group”, but rather it is “the social organisation of women’s reproductive and productive labor in different socioeconomic and cultural circumstances shapes the immediate ‘factors’ associated with women’s higher risks and climate change-related burdens” (Pearse, 2017) . It was the first time I had actually ever encountered significant disagreement in a team project, and so opted to follow the advice of “The Secrets of Great Teamwork” by Martine Haas and Mark Mortensen; that structuring norms for discussion can help greatly in making it efficient and effective (Haas and Mortsen, 2016). Unlike the hour long discussion of the topic online, where no compromise was even remotely reached, by setting up a time and place to meet and talk in person we were able to straighten out our differences within a matter of minutes. We realised that we were all basically on the same view of how to frame the issue, but just communicated this view differently. From this point, rehearsing and presenting our presentation went well as we became able to rely on a bond that lasted through some form of hardship.

Week two was also the most bittersweet time for us as a cohort. Knowing that we were all soon going to return to a time where the spontaneous adventures aren’t as easily ready for us was of course sad. Throughout the past two weeks I consciously made the effort to challenge my social anxiety. I used the opportunity I had of being surrounded by peers with the same interests and attitude towards social justice to make amazing memories in an equally amazing city with equally amazing people. Now I have gained life long friendships and memories that I will treasure forever, and am forever bound to Mumbai with a hunger for more.














•Conclusion•

Before I left for India I pictured that my first entry would illustrate India’s iconic beauty; idolising the way India’s radiant sun warms the streets at dawn and gently brings to life its vivid colours like a blooming daffodil. Now as I am writing this, a week since my return, I believe that my view of my GIG experience has become somewhat more nuanced- though I’m sure it will be subject to change throughout the rest of my life. The people of Mumbai have shown me that the city’s beauty, though outwardly subdued and its colours dulled slightly, is as bright as ever and indeed, is kept alive by the spirits of its populous. The people I met in Mumbai have been the most generous and kind people I have possibly ever met. But more than this, they are simultaneously also the most fierce and resilient people I have possibly ever met. Truthfully the requirements for this conclusion are also impossible to condense into 200 words. But with everything I’ve learnt and accomplished, the same lesson recurs: "keep exploring”. Keep exploring your realm of understanding, the corners of a city and its millions of menus, friendships, the limits of your comfort zone; there is so much to gain from exploration focused mindset and I hope that I continue to make an effort to always be exploring.
